Introduction

Rabivy is AX Pharmaceutical’s obesity asset with a monthly (or less frequent) dosing regimen and cardiometabolic positioning. As a pre-launch asset, no real-world prescribing data yet exists. Commercial analytics teams must therefore develop robust methods to identify which Healthcare Professionals (HCPs) are most likely to prescribe Rabivy at launch and to understand the key drivers of early adoption. The closest benchmarks are the launches of semaglutide (Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Zepbound).

The primary objective of this analysis is to build and validate a propensity model that predicts which HCPs are likely to prescribe Rabivy in the first 12 weeks post-launch.

Data Generation

Because no real prescribing labels exist, we developed a fully synthetic dataset of 5,000 HCPs. The data-generating process (DGP) was explicitly constructed to reflect realistic clinical and commercial drivers observed in analogous GLP-1 launches. Key features were simulated across prescription behaviour, patient panel characteristics, payer/access dynamics, and rep engagement. A binary outcome (“likely to prescribe”) was generated from a known ground-truth function, allowing rigorous validation that models recover the planted signals.

Modelling Approach

We evaluated three complementary modelling strategies:

  1. Logistic Regression – A transparent, interpretable baseline using main effects.

  2. XGBoost – A gradient boosting model that captures non-linear relationships and interactions, with strong explainability via SHAP.

  3. Feed-Forward Neural Network (MLP) – A deep learning model implemented in keras3 (TensorFlow) to explore more complex pattern learning.

All models were trained on the same set of engineered features and evaluated on a held-out test set (30%).

Evaluation Framework

To ensure robust and unbiased assessment, we implemented a structured evaluation harness using a golden test set — a 30% hold-out portion of the data that was completely withheld from model training and hyperparameter tuning. This approach simulates how the models would perform on future, unseen HCPs.

  • All models were evaluated on the golden test set using a consistent set of metrics:

  • Discrimination: AUC and Gini coefficient.

  • Balanced performance: Balanced Accuracy, Sensitivity, and Specificity.

  • Precision: The proportion of HCPs predicted as likely to prescribe who actually were (Positive Predictive Value).

  • Calibration: Brier Score and visual calibration plots (predicted vs. observed rates).

  • Signal recovery: Comparison of feature importance against the planted ground-truth drivers from the DGP.

This multi-metric harness provides a comprehensive view of both predictive power and practical usability, allowing confident model selection for commercial deployment.

Simulation

Variable 1 - HCP Specialty

Specialty is the foundational variable in the simulation; all downstream variables are drawn conditional on it. Three prescriber types are included: Primary Care, Endocrinology, and Obesity Medicine.

Probabilities are calibrated to real-world GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1) prescribing data from analogous launches (semaglutide/Wegovy, tirzepatide/Zepbound). BCBS Blue Health Intelligence analysis of weight-management GLP-1 prescriptions found that fewer than 10% of scripts were written by endocrinologists and obesity medicine specialists combined, with the large majority originating from primary care settings. A Northwestern Medicine longitudinal study found primary care’s share of first-time GLP-1 prescriptions grew from 8% in 2013 to 57% by 2019 — a trend that has continued.

Assumptions: Primary Care 82% / Endocrinology 10% / Obesity Medicine 8%

Variable 2 - Monthly GLP-1 Rx Volume per HCP

Total GLP-1 scripts written per HCP per month across all brands. Drawn from a negative binomial distribution, which captures the characteristic right-skew of prescriber volume: most HCPs are low-frequency, with a small tail of high-volume prescribers.

Average volume differs by specialty to reflect how concentrated each doctor’s patient panel is around GLP-1-eligible patients. Obesity medicine specialists are assumed to write the most (assumption -> mean ~60/month), as nearly their entire practice is weight-management focused. Endocrinologists sit in the middle (assumption -> mean ~35/month), prescribing for both diabetes and obesity. Primary care doctors write the fewest individually (assumption -> mean ~12/month), though they make up the bulk of the prescriber population. These figures are assumptions - per-HCP volume data is proprietary to IQVIA Xponent and not publicly reported.

Approximately 60% of primary care HCPs in the simulation are zero-writers - this is an assumption - they hold a relevant specialty but have not yet adopted GLP-1 prescribing. Endocrinologists and obesity medicine specialists are excluded from zero-inflation, as they are highly likely to prescribe GLP-1s given their patient population.

Assumptions: mean scripts/month by specialty - Obesity Med ~60, Endo ~35, Primary Care ~12; zero-inflated at 60% for primary care HCPs only.

Variable 4 - Payer Mix

Captures what share of a HCP’s GLP-1 patients fall into each payer category: Commercial, Medicare, Medicaid, or out-of-pocket. This is conditional on specialty, reflecting differences in typical patient population - obesity medicine clinics see more cash-pay patients, while primary care serves a broader payer mix including more government insurance.

These figures are educated assumptions, set against a backdrop of real but shifting policy: Medicaid coverage of GLP-1s for obesity is currently optional and has been narrowing (13 states covering as of January 2026, down from 16 in 2025), while a new Medicare GLP-1 Bridge demonstration program is set to begin in July 2026, temporarily opening limited obesity-indication coverage. This volatility means payer mix at Rabivy’s eventual launch is genuinely uncertain and should be revisited closer to launch.

Assumptions: Commercial/Medicare/Medicaid/OOP - Primary Care 30/40/20/10, Endocrinology 50/30/12/8, Obesity Medicine 55/18/7/20.

Variable 6 - Prior Authorization Burden

Estimates the likelihood that a GLP-1 prescription requires prior authorization, based on the HCP’s dominant payer type. Drawn from a Beta distribution per payer, which captures realistic variation in PA burden rather than a single fixed rate.

These are educated assumptions: Medicare and Medicaid are weighted toward higher PA burden, consistent with the current obesity-coverage landscape where both programs only recently began (or are about to begin) covering GLP-1s for obesity, typically with strict gatekeeping. Out-of-pocket patients face no PA burden.

Assumptions: PA rate (mean) - Commercial ~40%, Medicare ~55%, Medicaid ~60%, OOP 0%.

Variable 8 - Representative Engagement Recency

Captures how recently an AXPharmaceuticals rep has engaged with this HCP, decayed over time - a recent contact carries a strong signal, a contact six months ago carries almost none. Targeting status is now conditional on two factors: prescribing volume (reps prioritize high-volume writers) and existing AXPharmaceuticals relationship (reps prioritize warmer, pre-existing relationships). HCPs ranking high on both are most likely to be actively targeted; cold, low-volume HCPs are least likely.

Days since last contact are drawn from an Exponential distribution - targeted HCPs average ~30 days since contact, non-targeted HCPs average ~120 days. This is converted to a decay score (0.97^days), so very recent contact scores near 1, and contact from months ago decays toward 0.

This is a pure internal assumption - actual Veeva/CRM rep engagement data isn’t publicly available, but the logic that targeting follows volume and relationship strength is a reasonable commercial behavior pattern.

Assumptions: Targeting probability scaled by volume percentile + AXPharmaceuticals relationship tier; targeted mean 30 days since contact, non-targeted mean 120 days; decay rate 0.97 per day.

Show code 
# V8: Rep Engagement Recency, conditional on volume + AXPharmaceuticals relationship

# Targeting score: volume percentile + relationship tier
volume_pctile <- rank(hcp_df$rx_volume_monthly) / n

relationship_weight <- ifelse(
  hcp_df$AXPharmaceuticals_relationship == "TwoPlus", 1.0,
  ifelse(hcp_df$AXPharmaceuticals_relationship == "One", 0.5, 0)
)

targeting_score <- 0.6 * volume_pctile + 0.4 * relationship_weight

# Convert to targeting probability (calibrated so ~40% end up targeted)
targeting_prob <- targeting_score / max(targeting_score) * 0.7  # scaling factor tuned for ~40% targeted
hcp_df$targeted <- rbinom(n, size = 1, prob = targeting_prob)

# Days since last contact, conditional on targeted status
hcp_df$days_since_contact <- ifelse(
  hcp_df$targeted == 1,
  rexp(n, rate = 1/30),
  rexp(n, rate = 1/120)
)

# Decay score
hcp_df$rep_engagement_score <- 0.97 ^ hcp_df$days_since_contact

Variable 10 - Years in Practice

No strong specialty gradient is imposed because the effect is expected to operate similarly across primary care, endocrinology, and obesity medicine once other factors are held constant.

Assumptions: Mean 18 years (SD 8), truncated 5–40 years; slight negative effect on propensity after adjustment for volume and access.

Obesity prevalence in a HCP’s patient panel is one of the strongest clinical signals of Rabivy eligibility. HCPs whose panels contain a high proportion of patients with obesity have a much larger addressable population for a new obesity-indicated GLP-1.

This variable is simulated conditional on specialty to reflect real differences in patient mix: obesity medicine specialists see the highest concentration, followed by endocrinologists, with primary care showing the broadest but lower average prevalence.

Higher obesity prevalence is expected to be a major positive driver of Rabivy prescribing propensity, especially when combined with high new-start volume and favourable access. This variable captures the core clinical opportunity that commercial targeting models must identify.

Dependent Variable: Binary Prescribing Outcome

Because Rabivy has not yet launched, no real-world prescribing labels exist. We therefore simulate the binary outcome (“likely to prescribe Rabivy in the first 12 weeks post-launch”) from an explicit, fully transparent data-generating process (DGP). This approach is deliberate and methodologically strong for a pre-launch commercial analytics project: it allows us to plant realistic clinical and commercial drivers and later demonstrate that our models recover those same drivers rather than spurious patterns.

The DGP constructs a latent propensity on the logit scale as a linear combination of the most important features. Coefficients are chosen to reflect both published evidence from analogous GLP-1 launches and standard commercial targeting logic:

Strongest positive drivers (largest coefficients):

  • Monthly GLP-1 prescribing volume and new-start share (high-volume, early-adopter HCPs are the clearest early prescribers of a new agent; Huskamp et al., 2013).

  • Obesity prevalence in the patient panel (core clinical eligibility signal).

  • Favourable formulary access and low prior-authorisation burden (access friction is one of the largest real-world barriers in the current GLP-1 market).

  • Obesity Medicine or Endocrinology specialty (concentrated eligible patient panels).

Moderate positive drivers:

  • Existing relationship with AX Pharmaceuticals (trust and familiarity with the company’s cardiometabolic portfolio).

  • Recent rep engagement and recent sample request (detailing + active interest effects).

  • Commercial or cash-pay dominant payer mix (lower access friction on average).

  • Higher academic engagement

Stratifying / modest negative driver:

  • Years in practice (more experienced and older physicians tend to adopt new therapies more slowly once volume and access are controlled).

Moderate Gaussian noise is added so the relationship between features and outcome is strong but imperfect mirroring real-world prescribing decisions. The resulting positive rate (~22%) is calibrated to be realistic for early post-launch adoption of a new monthly GLP-1 in a competitive market.

This transparent DGP is the foundation for all subsequent model evaluation - we can test whether each model correctly ranks the planted drivers at the top of feature importance (Rainio et al., 2024). Models that pass statistical tests but fail to recover the known ground truth are of limited value for commercial decision-making.

Evaluation of Model Performance

  • AUC (Area Under the ROC Curve): Measures the model’s ability to discriminate between HCPs who are likely vs. unlikely to prescribe Rabivy. An AUC of 0.5 indicates random guessing; 1 indicates perfect discrimination.

  • Gini Coefficient: Calculated as 2 × AUC – 1. A common industry metric that expresses discriminatory power as a percentage (0% = random, 100% = perfect).

  • Accuracy: The overall proportion of correct predictions (both likely and unlikely prescribers).

  • Balanced Accuracy: The average of sensitivity and specificity.

  • Sensitivity (Recall): The proportion of actual high-propensity HCPs that the model correctly identifies (true positive rate).

  • Specificity: The proportion of actual low-propensity HCPs that the model correctly identifies (true negative rate).

  • Precision (Positive Predictive Value): The proportion of HCPs predicted as likely to prescribe who actually are likely to prescribe. High precision means fewer false positives when targeting.

  • Brier Score: A calibration metric that measures the accuracy of predicted probabilities. It is the mean squared difference between predicted probabilities and actual outcomes. Lower values indicate better calibration.

According to Rainio and colleagues (2024), accuracy, sensitivity, specificity and precision are the most widely used evaluation metrics for binary classification.

Model 3: Deep Learning Model (Multi-Layer Perceptron)

As a final modeling step, we implement a feed-forward neural network (also known as a Multi-Layer Perceptron or MLP) using the keras3 package, which runs on top of TensorFlow.

How the MLP Works:

The neural network consists of multiple layers of interconnected “neurons”:

  • An input layer that receives all our engineered features.

  • Several hidden layers (128 → 64 → 32 neurons in this model) where the network learns complex, non-linear combinations and interactions between features.

  • An output layer that produces a single probability (0 to 1) of the HCP being likely to prescribe Rabivy, using a sigmoid activation function.

During training, the model adjusts millions of internal weights through backpropagation to minimize prediction error. Dropout layers are included to prevent overfitting. Compared to XGBoost, MLP can learn more complex hierarchical patterns and interactions through its layered structure.

Note on Reproducibility:

Keras/TensorFlow models involve an internal random number generator. Although we applied multiple reproducibility controls, minor run-to-run variations may still occur. This is a known characteristic of deep learning frameworks. All results presented represent a representative run under fixed seeding conditions.

Permutation Feature Importance

Unlike XGBoost or logistic regression, neural networks do not have a direct measure of feature importance. To address this, we used Permutation Feature Importance which directly quantifies how much each feature contributes to the model’s ability to discriminate between likely and unlikely prescribers.

Show code 
# ====================== PERMUTATION IMPORTANCE ======================

perm_imp <- function(model, X_test, y_test, n_perm = 10) {
  baseline_auc <- roc(y_test, as.numeric(predict(model, X_test, verbose = 0)))$auc
  
  importance <- numeric(ncol(X_test))
  names(importance) <- colnames(X_test)
  
  for (i in 1:ncol(X_test)) {
    auc_drop <- numeric(n_perm)
    for (p in 1:n_perm) {
      X_perm <- X_test
      X_perm[, i] <- sample(X_perm[, i])
      pred_perm <- as.numeric(predict(model, X_perm, verbose = 0))
      auc_drop[p] <- baseline_auc - roc(y_test, pred_perm)$auc
    }
    importance[i] <- mean(auc_drop)
  }
  
  sort(importance, decreasing = TRUE)
}

Summary and Conclusion

Understanding the factors that influence HCPs adoption of new medications is essential for pharmaceutical companies, medical aid schemes, and policy makers (Huskamp et al., 2013). This analysis developed a robust propensity scoring model to identify which Healthcare Professionals are most likely to prescribe Rabivy at launch. Using a fully synthetic dataset of 5,000 HCPs and a transparent, ground-truth data-generating process, we engineered clinically and commercially meaningful features grounded in real-world GLP-1 launch dynamics. We then evaluated and compared the performance of three different models using established evaluation metrics for binary classification (Rainio et al., 2024).

All three models performed strongly on the held-out test set:

  • Logistic Regression: AUC 0.9183 (Gini 83.7%)

  • XGBoost: AUC 0.912 (Gini 82.4%)

  • Feed-Forward Neural Network (MLP): AUC 0.9022 (Gini 80.4%)

The models demonstrated excellent discrimination (all AUC > 0.90), good calibration, and consistent recovery of the planted drivers from the data-generating process.

Across all three modelling approaches, a clear and consistent set of drivers emerged. Obesity prevalence in the patient panel, representative engagement, GLP-1 prescribing volume, formulary access, and prior authorization burden were the dominant predictors of early adoption intent. This cross-model agreement increases confidence that these factors are genuinely important rather than artefacts of any single modelling technique.

Key Commercial Insights

  • Prioritize HCPs with high obesity prevalence among their patients and high GLP-1 volume/new-start activity.

  • Ensure strong formulary positioning and actively work to minimize prior authorization friction.

  • Invest in relationship-building and maintain active rep engagement, particularly with high-potential specialists and academically engaged prescribers.

Next Steps

While the models performed well on the synthetic test set, several important next steps remain before any real-world deployment:

  • Post-training evaluation on unseen data: The current evaluation was performed on a held-out portion of the synthetic dataset. The true test will be to expose the model to a completely new dataset that has not been seen during development. This will help assess how the model generalises when the underlying data distribution shifts.

  • Parameter drift and distribution shift: Model performance is only as good as the sample on which it was developed. It is important to monitor how the model behaves when key relationships in the data change.

  • Ground truth evolution: In a real deployment, the definition of “likely to prescribe” can change over time. Continuous monitoring and periodic model retraining will be necessary to ensure the model remains relevant as market conditions evolve.

## Introduction

Rabivy is AX Pharmaceutical's obesity asset with a monthly (or less frequent) dosing regimen and cardiometabolic positioning. As a pre-launch asset, no real-world prescribing data yet exists. Commercial analytics teams must therefore develop robust methods to identify which Healthcare Professionals (HCPs) are most likely to prescribe Rabivy at launch and to understand the key drivers of early adoption. The closest benchmarks are the launches of semaglutide (Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Zepbound). 

The primary objective of this analysis is to build and validate a propensity model that predicts which HCPs are likely to prescribe Rabivy in the first 12 weeks post-launch. 

## Data Generation 

Because no real prescribing labels exist, we developed a fully synthetic dataset of 5,000 HCPs. The data-generating process (DGP) was explicitly constructed to reflect realistic clinical and commercial drivers observed in analogous GLP-1 launches. Key features were simulated across prescription behaviour, patient panel characteristics, payer/access dynamics, and rep engagement. A binary outcome (“likely to prescribe”) was generated from a known ground-truth function, allowing rigorous validation that models recover the planted signals.

## Modelling Approach 

We evaluated three complementary modelling strategies:

1. Logistic Regression – A transparent, interpretable baseline using main effects.

2. XGBoost – A gradient boosting model that captures non-linear relationships and interactions, with strong explainability via SHAP.

3. Feed-Forward Neural Network (MLP) – A deep learning model implemented in keras3 (TensorFlow) to explore more complex pattern learning.

All models were trained on the same set of engineered features and evaluated on a held-out test set (30%).

## Evaluation Framework

To ensure robust and unbiased assessment, we implemented a structured **evaluation harness** using a **golden test set** — a 30% hold-out portion of the data that was completely withheld from model training and hyperparameter tuning. This approach simulates how the models would perform on future, unseen HCPs.

- All models were evaluated on the golden test set using a consistent set of metrics:

- Discrimination: AUC and Gini coefficient.

- Balanced performance: Balanced Accuracy, Sensitivity, and Specificity.

- Precision: The proportion of HCPs predicted as likely to prescribe who actually were (Positive Predictive Value).

- Calibration: Brier Score and visual calibration plots (predicted vs. observed rates).

- Signal recovery: Comparison of feature importance against the planted ground-truth drivers from the DGP.

This multi-metric harness provides a comprehensive view of both predictive power and practical usability, allowing confident model selection for commercial deployment.

### Variable 8 - Representative Engagement Recency
Captures how recently an AXPharmaceuticals rep has engaged with this HCP, decayed over time - a recent contact carries a strong signal, a contact six months ago carries almost none. Targeting status is now conditional on two factors: prescribing volume (reps prioritize high-volume writers) and existing AXPharmaceuticals relationship (reps prioritize warmer, pre-existing relationships). Days since last contact are drawn from an Exponential distribution - targeted HCPs average ~30 days since contact, non-targeted HCPs average ~120 days. This is converted to a decay score (0.97^days), so very recent contact scores near 1, and contact from months ago decays toward 0.

This is a pure internal assumption - actual Veeva/CRM rep engagement data isn't publicly available, but the logic that targeting follows volume and relationship strength is a reasonable commercial behavior pattern.

**Assumptions:** Targeting probability scaled by volume percentile + AXPharmaceuticals relationship tier; targeted mean 30 days since contact, non-targeted mean 120 days; decay rate 0.97 per day.

### Dependent Variable: Binary Prescribing Outcome
Because Rabivy has not yet launched, no real-world prescribing labels exist. We therefore simulate the binary outcome (“likely to prescribe Rabivy in the first 12 weeks post-launch”) from an explicit, fully transparent data-generating process (DGP). This approach is deliberate and methodologically strong for a pre-launch commercial analytics project: it allows us to plant realistic clinical and commercial drivers and later demonstrate that our models recover those same drivers rather than spurious patterns.

The DGP constructs a latent propensity on the logit scale as a linear combination of the most important features. Coefficients are chosen to reflect both published evidence from analogous GLP-1 launches and standard commercial targeting logic:

**Strongest positive drivers (largest coefficients):**

- Monthly GLP-1 prescribing volume and new-start share (high-volume, early-adopter HCPs are the clearest early prescribers of a new agent; Huskamp et al., 2013). 

- Obesity prevalence in the patient panel (core clinical eligibility signal).

- Favourable formulary access and low prior-authorisation burden (access friction is one of the largest real-world barriers in the current GLP-1 market).

- Obesity Medicine or Endocrinology specialty (concentrated eligible patient panels).

**Moderate positive drivers:**

- Existing relationship with AX Pharmaceuticals (trust and familiarity with the company’s cardiometabolic portfolio).

- Recent rep engagement and recent sample request (detailing + active interest effects).

- Commercial or cash-pay dominant payer mix (lower access friction on average). 

- Higher academic engagement 

**Stratifying / modest negative driver:**

- Years in practice (more experienced and older physicians tend to adopt new therapies more slowly once volume and access are controlled).

Moderate Gaussian noise is added so the relationship between features and outcome is strong but imperfect mirroring real-world prescribing decisions. The resulting positive rate (~22%) is calibrated to be realistic for early post-launch adoption of a new monthly GLP-1 in a competitive market.

This transparent DGP is the foundation for all subsequent model evaluation - we can test whether each model correctly ranks the planted drivers at the top of feature importance (Rainio et al., 2024). Models that pass statistical tests but fail to recover the known ground truth are of limited value for commercial decision-making. 

```{r}

hcp_df$obesity_prev_z    <- scale(hcp_df$obesity_prev)[,1]
hcp_df$pa_burden_z       <- scale(hcp_df$pa_burden)[,1]
hcp_df$rep_engagement_z  <- scale(hcp_df$rep_engagement_score)[,1]
hcp_df$years_practice_z  <- scale(hcp_df$years_practice)[,1]
hcp_df$academic_engagement_z  <- scale(hcp_df$academic_engagement)[,1]

# Formulary access score
formulary_score <- case_when(
  hcp_df$formulary_tier == "Preferred"    ~  2.0,
  hcp_df$formulary_tier == "NonPreferred" ~  0.8,
  hcp_df$formulary_tier == "PARequired"   ~ -0.6,
  TRUE ~ -1.8
)
hcp_df$formulary_z <- scale(formulary_score)[,1]

# AX Pharmaceuticals relationship (ordered numeric)
AXPharmaceuticals_relationship_num <- ifelse(hcp_df$AXPharmaceuticals_relationship == "TwoPlus", 2,
                                 ifelse(hcp_df$AXPharmaceuticals_relationship == "One", 1, 0))
hcp_df$AXPharmaceuticals_relationship_z <- scale(AXPharmaceuticals_relationship_num)[,1]

# Specialty effect
specialty_effect <- ifelse(
  hcp_df$specialty == "Obesity Medicine", 1.35,
  ifelse(hcp_df$specialty == "Endocrinology", 0.72, 0)
```

## Evaluation of Model Performance 

- **AUC (Area Under the ROC Curve):** Measures the model’s ability to discriminate between HCPs who are likely vs. unlikely to prescribe Rabivy. An AUC of 0.5 indicates random guessing; 1 indicates perfect discrimination.

- **Gini Coefficient:** Calculated as `2 × AUC – 1`. A common industry metric that expresses discriminatory power as a percentage (0% = random, 100% = perfect).

- **Accuracy:** The overall proportion of correct predictions (both likely and unlikely prescribers).

- **Balanced Accuracy:** The average of sensitivity and specificity. 

- **Sensitivity (Recall):** The proportion of actual high-propensity HCPs that the model correctly identifies (true positive rate).

- **Specificity:** The proportion of actual low-propensity HCPs that the model correctly identifies (true negative rate).

- **Precision (Positive Predictive Value)**: The proportion of HCPs predicted as likely to prescribe who actually are likely to prescribe. High precision means fewer false positives when targeting. 

- **Brier Score**: A calibration metric that measures the accuracy of predicted probabilities. It is the mean squared difference between predicted probabilities and actual outcomes. Lower values indicate better calibration. 

According to Rainio and colleagues (2024), accuracy, sensitivity, specificity and precision are the most widely used evaluation metrics for binary classification.

### Model 3: Deep Learning Model (Multi-Layer Perceptron)

As a final modeling step, we implement a feed-forward neural network (also known as a Multi-Layer Perceptron or MLP) using the `keras3` package, which runs on top of TensorFlow. 

**How the MLP Works:**

The neural network consists of multiple layers of interconnected "neurons":

- An input layer that receives all our engineered features.

- Several hidden layers (128 → 64 → 32 neurons in this model) where the network learns complex, non-linear combinations and interactions between features.

- An output layer that produces a single probability (0 to 1) of the HCP being likely to prescribe Rabivy, using a sigmoid activation function.

During training, the model adjusts millions of internal weights through backpropagation to minimize prediction error. Dropout layers are included to prevent overfitting. Compared to XGBoost, MLP can learn more complex hierarchical patterns and interactions through its layered structure.

Keras/TensorFlow models involve an internal random number generator. Although we applied multiple reproducibility controls, minor run-to-run variations may still occur. perm_imp <- function(model, X_test, y_test, n_perm = 10) {
```{r}
  baseline_auc <- roc(y_test, as.numeric(predict(model, X_test, verbose = 0)))$auc
  
  importance <- numeric(ncol(X_test))
  names(importance) <- colnames(X_test)
  
  for (i in 1:ncol(X_test)) {
    auc_drop <- numeric(n_perm)
    for (p in 1:n_perm) {
      X_perm <- X_test
      X_perm[, i] <- sample(X_perm[, i])
      pred_perm <- as.numeric(predict(model, X_perm, verbose = 0))
      auc_drop[p] <- baseline_auc - roc(y_test, pred_perm)$auc
    }
    importance[i] <- mean(auc_drop)
  }
  
  sort(importance, decreasing = TRUE)
}
```

To improve the reliability of the predicted probabilities from the feed-forward neural network, we applied Platt scaling — a post-hoc calibration technique. This method fits a logistic regression model on the raw output probabilities from the neural network (using the training data) and then uses this model to adjust the probabilities on the test set. The goal is to produce better-calibrated probabilities without changing the model’s ranking ability. 

## Summary and Conclusion 

Understanding the factors that influence HCPs adoption of new medications is essential for pharmaceutical companies, medical aid schemes, and policy makers (Huskamp et al., 2013). This analysis developed a robust propensity scoring model to identify which Healthcare Professionals are most likely to prescribe Rabivy at launch. Using a fully synthetic dataset of 5,000 HCPs and a transparent, ground-truth data-generating process, we engineered clinically and commercially meaningful features grounded in real-world GLP-1 launch dynamics. We then evaluated and compared the performance of three different models using established evaluation metrics for binary classification (Rainio et al., 2024). 

All three models performed strongly on the held-out test set:

- Logistic Regression: AUC 0.9183 (Gini 83.7%)

- XGBoost: AUC 0.912 (Gini 82.4%)

- Feed-Forward Neural Network (MLP): AUC 0.9022 (Gini 80.4%)

The models demonstrated excellent discrimination (all AUC > 0.90), good calibration, and consistent recovery of the planted drivers from the data-generating process. 

Across all three modelling approaches, a clear and consistent set of drivers emerged. Obesity prevalence in the patient panel, representative engagement, GLP-1 prescribing volume, formulary access, and prior authorization burden were the dominant predictors of early adoption intent. This cross-model agreement increases confidence that these factors are genuinely important rather than artefacts of any single modelling technique.

### Key Commercial Insights

- Prioritize HCPs with high obesity prevalence among their patients and high GLP-1 volume/new-start activity.

- Ensure strong formulary positioning and actively work to minimize prior authorization friction.

- Invest in relationship-building and maintain active rep engagement, particularly with high-potential specialists and academically engaged prescribers. 

### Next Steps 

While the models performed well on the synthetic test set, several important next steps remain before any real-world deployment:

- Post-training evaluation on unseen data: The current evaluation was performed on a held-out portion of the synthetic dataset. The true test will be to expose the model to a completely new dataset that has not been seen during development. This will help assess how the model generalises when the underlying data distribution shifts.

- Parameter drift and distribution shift: Model performance is only as good as the sample on which it was developed. It is important to monitor how the model behaves when key relationships in the data change.

- Ground truth evolution: In a real deployment, the definition of “likely to prescribe” can change over time. Continuous monitoring and periodic model retraining will be necessary to ensure the model remains relevant as market conditions evolve.
